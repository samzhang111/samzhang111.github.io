Unfolding Polytopes

By Sam Zhang with Matt Harvey and Satyan Devadoss
Intuitively, we all know how to unfold a three-dimensional polytope. Cut along its edges, and then lay the faces flat in two-dimensional space. When the faces don't overlap in the plane, we call the resulting pattern a net.
We can do this with four-dimensional polytopes as well. The generalization of an edge is called a "ridge", and the resulting "ridge unfolding" produces a beautiful footprint in three-dimensional space. In four dimensions, the cube, simplex, and orthoplex all unfold into nets.
